中文全文

本篇新聞彙整呈現全球水、衛生、健康、氣候與基礎設施領域的多重壓力。首先，亞洲開發銀行的《Asian Water Development Outlook 2025》指出，亞太地區在過去十二年間已有超過 27 億人脫離極端水不安全狀態，但這些進展正受到生態系退化、氣候風險與資金缺口威脅。若要在 2040 年前滿足區域供水、衛生與個人衛生服務需求，每年仍需約 2500 億美元投資。報告強調，濕地、河川、含水層與森林等支撐長期水安全的自然資本正快速惡化；極端天氣、海平面上升、風暴潮與鹽水入侵也正加劇風險。ADB 呼籲恢復生態系健康、強化韌性、改善水治理，並透過混合融資與更具效率的水部門財務模式縮小投資缺口。 第二則新聞指出，新的研究發現，當降雨主要來自陸地蒸發而非海洋水氣時，作物對乾旱更為脆弱。研究團隊利用二十年的衛星資料追蹤大氣水分來源，發現若約三分之一降雨來自土壤、湖泊與森林等陸地來源，農地土壤水分會下降，作物產量也會降低。美國中西部與東非特別暴露於此風險，原因包括土壤乾化與森林砍伐。研究者因此呼籲保護森林並改善土地管理，以降低農業水風險。 公共衛生方面，UNICEF 警告剛果民主共和國正面臨 25 年來最嚴重的霍亂疫情。2025 年通報病例超過 6.4 萬例，死亡近 1900 人，兒童約占四分之一。疫情受限於安全飲水與基本衛生服務不足、東部衝突與流離失所、強降雨與洪水破壞 WASH 基礎設施，以及快速且缺乏規劃的都市化。政府雖已提出 2023-2027 多部門消除霍亂計畫，但資金不足，並另啟動「無霍亂剛果河」倡議，針對港口、船隻衛生、船員與乘客宣導及沿河飲水取得進行改善。 短訊部分包括：美國加州在多年乾旱後首度全州脫離乾旱狀態；歐盟飲用水中 PFAS 的系統性監測與限值規範正式生效；Ramboll 併購北歐水與廢水專業公司 Envidan，擴大全球水業務能力。環境與氣候新聞則指出，UNEP GEO-7 報告認為投資穩定氣候、健康自然與無污染地球，可帶來額外全球 GDP、避免數百萬人死亡並降低貧困與飢餓；WWF 報告警告美國極端天氣正在擴大保險保障缺口，需投資自然解方以降低洪水、熱浪與風暴損害；WMO 首份阿拉伯地區氣候報告指出 2024 年為該區有紀錄以來最熱年份，升溫、熱浪、乾旱、強降雨與風暴正與都市化、衝突、貧困和人口成長交織；冰川研究則預測二十一世紀中葉可能出現冰川消失高峰。最後，城市水安全圖譜顯示全球前一百大城市中有一半面臨高水壓力，並介紹 NX Filtration 與 Jaya Teknik Indonesia 合作、以及 IDE Technologies 獲得孟買海水淡化工程合約等產業消息。整體而言，這組新聞共同說明水安全已與自然資本、公共衛生、氣候韌性、金融保險、城市治理與基礎設施投資緊密相連。

aww Uw ADB report calls for $250 billion a year for Asian WASH services More than 60% of Asia and the Pacific’s population - about 2.7 billion people - has been lifted from extreme water insecurity over the past 12 years, but these hard- won gains are threatened by accelerating environmental decline and a major financing gap, according to a new Asian Development Bank (ADB) report. Asian Water Development Outlook (AWDO) 2025 finds that renewed political commitment, targeted investments and governance reforms since 2013 have driven this progress. But ecosystem decline, climate risks and funding shortfalls for water investments threaten to plunge billions back into water insecurity. The report highlights that wetlands, rivers, aquifers, and forests that sustain long-term water security are deteriorating fast. With extreme weather events, such as storm surges, rising sea levels and saltwater intrusion threatening a region that already accounts for 41% of the world’s floods. The AWDO estimates that $4 trillion Environment through to 2040 - or $250 billion a year - is needed to meet the region’s water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH) needs alone. ADB senior director for water and urban development Norio Saito said: “Asia’s water story is a tale of two realities, with monumental achievements on water security coupled with rising risks that could undermine this progress. Without water security, there is no development. This report shows we need to act urgently to restore ecosystem health, strengthen resilience, improve water governance and deploy innovative finance to deliver long-term water security - especially for the neediest communities.” The region’s steady improvement over the past 12 years is starkly demonstrated in rural India, where 840 million people now have better WASH services, due chiefly to government programmes. In Tajikistan, projects supported by initiatives like the United Nations’ Early Warnings for All have reduced drought exposure and strengthened national flood and storm management systems. The AWDO notes that progress can be sustained if nature is protected and integrated into water systems through national river health monitoring, stronger pollution controls and better watershed protection. Stronger governance systems that prioritise prevention will improve climate resilience, and water systems will be more effective if subnational bodies are given the resources and authority to manage them. It adds that efforts must be inclusive - engaging women and young people - if investments are to succeed. The report finds that current spending on WASH infrastructure covers less than 40% of the estimated $250 billion needed every year, leaving an annual shortfall of more than $150 billion. Improving the efficiency and financial sustainability of the water sector can also attract private investment. New research has found that crops are more vulnerable to drought when rainfall originates from the land rather than the ocean. Researchers from the University of California San Diego, USA, used 20 years of satellite measurements to track atmospheric moisture back to the place where it first evaporated. The study, published in the journal Nature Sustainability, found that when approximately one third of precipitation originates from land - such as soil, lakes and forests - as opposed to ocean-derived moisture within large weather systems - such as atmospheric rivers, monsoons and tropical storms - croplands become significantly more susceptible to drought, soil moisture declines and yields drop. The study found that crops in the Midwest of the USA and East Africa are particularly exposed because of soil drying and deforestation. The researchers call for action to protect forests and improve land management.
