當水本身成為武器
When water itself becomes a weapon

Jorge Rodriguez argues that water professionals have ethical duties when water infrastructure is deliberately denied, polluted or weaponised.

← 返回目錄 / Back to contents

中文完整摘要說明

本文討論當水被蓄意剝奪、污染或作為傷害工具時，水專業人員是否仍能自稱中立。作者指出，水維繫生命與尊嚴；若供水、處理廠、管線或基礎設施被用來傷害平民，這不只是技術問題，也是道德與法律問題。文章連結國際人道法、人權法與專業倫理，主張水領域應建立「Water Stewardship」倫理，將保護水權與避免技術中立淪為共犯納入專業身份。

中文全文

本文從水的倫理意義出發，指出水維繫生命、承載尊嚴，也可能成為健康與絕望之間的界線。工程師、科學家與水務專業人員常把自己描述為技術專業者，與政治或人道衝突保持距離。但當水被蓄意剝奪、污染或用來造成傷害時，專業中立是否仍然成立，便成為必須面對的問題。 文章指出，近年世界多次見到水被武器化：處理廠被轟炸、管線被切斷、物資運送路線被阻擋。這些行為不只是破壞基礎設施，而是拆解維繫平民生活的系統。若清潔水的剝奪成為系統性行為，或透過人口隔離讓一群人享有穩定基礎設施、另一群人長期受限，便跨越了道德與法律門檻。 作者引用國際法中的種族滅絕、公約、種族隔離罪與國際刑事法院羅馬規約等原則，指出把水流中的不平等與剝奪工程化，不只是技術失敗，而是道德失敗。水專業人員不能只以數據、設計與證據為名，忽略自身系統可能被用於傷害而非治癒。 其他專業已面對過類似反省。醫學學會了中立可能變成殘酷，因此形成救治傷者的倫理；法律學會了程序若沒有良知會正當化不義；新聞學也形成見證責任。水領域也需要承認，專業若與倫理切割，可能成為共犯。 文章主張，國際法已承認水的保護，包括 Geneva Conventions 與 Additional Protocol I 對平民生存不可或缺物件的保護，以及聯合國承認安全清潔飲水與衛生為人權。但法律本身不足以保障實踐，水專業社群需要把法律原則轉化為活的道德準則。作者提出「Ethic of Water Stewardship」概念，主張剝奪或破壞取水權與水專業使命不相容。這種倫理不會使水專業政治化，而是使其扎根於人性。

Those of us — engineers, scientists and stewards who work with water — often portray ourselves as ‘technical professionals’, separate from the political or humanitarian turbulence of the world around us. Yet, when water is intentionally denied, polluted or used to cause harm, can we still claim neutrality? If our work is to protect life through water, then perhaps ethics should also flow through our profession. In recent years, the world has witnessed repeated instances of water being deliberately turned into a weapon — treatment plants bombed, pipelines severed and routes blocked to prevent delivery of essential materials. Such acts do not merely damage infrastructure, they dismantle the very systems that sustain civilian life. When the deprivation of clean water becomes systematic — whether through deliberate destruction or through the structural separation of populations, where one group enjoys continuous access to infrastructure, while another lives under chronic restriction — a moral and legal threshold is crossed. Among the gravest crimes in international law is ‘deliberately inflicting on the group conditions of life calculated to bring about its physical destruction in whole or in part’, as set out in the 1948 Genocide Convention'. International law also condemns institutionalised segregation and domination, as defined in the 1973 Apartheid Convention and the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court (Article 7)* >. For water professionals, these principles converge in a single truth: inequality and deprivation engineered into the flow of water are not technical failures — they are moral ones. The weaponisation of water confronts us — scientists, engineers and professionals — with an uncomfortable truth. Our field, though rooted in evidence and design, does not exist outside the moral boundaries of humanity. We must reflect on the consequences of silence when our “work, or the systems we help build, are used to harm rather than to heal.
